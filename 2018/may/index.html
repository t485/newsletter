April Backpacking - Post-Trip

Jacob Nie

The crew hiked 44 miles in 4 days of hiking in Big Sur’s Ventana Wilderness, some of the “wildest” places left in California. It was undoubtedly very challenging, and I think everybody pushed themselves to their limits. I’m very proud of everybody in the crew for showing unbelievable grit and relentless optimism on such a challenging trip.

I’ll share one particular anecdote that you may find interesting as a reader who did not attend. It was late afternoon, and we were close to topping out on one of the trip’s toughest uphill climbs. By tough, I mean obscured trails and lots of bushwhacking. (If you don’t know what bushwhacking is, just assume it means “something pretty hard to do.”) We were around 400 feet away from the next trail we were trying to hit, but we had lost the trail. Not only that, we could even see the trail (and the ocean) from where we were standing. How tantalizing it was to stand there and see our hopes separated from us by 100 yards of impenetrable brush! Henceforth, that strip of trail was (accurately) dubbed “The Promised Land.” We made it in the end, after Mr. Chen miraculously found the trail after nearly 2 hours of deliberating our next course action (i.e. giving up).

There were a lot of things to learn from this trip (one of which is to never go backpacking in Big Sur). I think this is a great stepping stone for our troop to attempt even greater adventures. Get ready for next year!

Philmont Shakedown - Reflection

Max Lian What went well:
  • We stayed on time, and didn’t return home late.
  • We were able to use liquid gas stoves, as will be expected at Philmont.
  • We hiked with a good pace.
  • The food was good.
What didn’t go well:
  • There was poison oak everywhere.
  • Crew 1 had a large dead tree blocking the trail.
  • Crew 2 took too long to get everybody to wake up and start cooking breakfast, and ended up leaving campsite half an hour late.
What to improve on:
  • The duty roster organization was not great.
  • A little more food for breakfast would have been more satisfying.

Mother and Son Reflection

Jeffrey Meng

This year, Mother and Son took place at Mount Diablo State Park. We got off to a bit of a late start, with some scouts showing up at 9:30, the time we were supposed to depart. The attendance was also disappointing: two nights before, around 23 scouts were on the headcounts. The day of the event, only 17 scouts were on the headcounts, and of those 17, only 12 showed up. After we arrived at Mount Diablo, everything went smoothly. Although forecasts suggested rain showers on Saturday, rain never came. We ate lunch, the were able to play many games together and the scouts and mothers had a very fun time bonding.

Metrics Project

Forest Yang

A problem we've been discussing lately is that of disparities in attendance and activity between patrols. Certain patrols tend to have plenty of senior scouts and parents consistently showing up, others not so much. Furthermore, it's been hard to make decisions regarding methods to address this issue, such as patrol shuffling and placement of cub scouts from family camp, because anecdotal evidence can be inconsistent and vague. For this reason, we have created a spreadsheet compiling each patrol's attendance data for junior scouts, senior scouts, and parents at events in the past year. We will make graphs to clearly and objectively visualize trends in data; for example, stacked bar charts comparing Blobfish junior, senior, and total scout attendance across events. We can then accordingly implement solutions!
